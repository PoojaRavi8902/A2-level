Lecture 1 — Threats to Computer Systems (17.1)

10 minutes
What are Threats?

A threat is any potential danger that can compromise confidentiality, integrity, or availability of systems or data.

Key categories of threats (expand for details)
  • Malware — Virus, Worm, Trojan, Spyware, Ransomware. (Explain differences)
  • Social Engineering — Phishing, Pretexting, Baiting.
  • Brute Force — Password guessing at scale.
  • DoS / DDoS — Overwhelm services to deny access.
  • Insider Threats — Accidental or malicious actions by trusted users.
Examples
  • ILOVEYOU (2000) — virus spread via email.
  • WannaCry (2017) — ransomware targeting hospitals and services.
  • Phishing emails pretending to be banks.
Teacher notes
  • Emphasize viruses vs worms: viruses need a host file; worms self-replicate across networks.
  • Use a recent news story to make threats tangible.
  • Discuss insider threats and real safeguards (logging, least privilege).

  • Program copies itself over network = Worm (Malware)
  • Staff accidentally deletes critical file = Insider Threat

Lecture 2 — Methods to Protect Systems and Data (17.2)

10 minutes
Overview

Use technical, procedural and human measures together to reduce risk.

Key methods (expand)
  • Authentication & Access Control — passwords, 2FA, permissions, least privilege.
  • Encryption — data at rest and in transit (HTTPS, VPN, AES).
  • Firewalls — hardware and software barriers controlling traffic.
  • Anti-malware — AV, anti-spyware, anti-ransomware and automatic updates.
  • Backup & Recovery — full/incremental/differential strategies and offsite backups.
  • Security Awareness — training to spot phishing and social engineering.
Examples
  • Banks using SSL/TLS for web encryption.
  • Google using 2FA for new-device logins.
  • Corporate hardware firewalls isolating internal networks.
Teacher notes
  • Stress layered defence.
  • Use analogies: firewall = security guard; encryption = coded letter; backup = spare key.

Lecture 3 — Cyber Security Policies, Legal & Ethical Issues (17.3)

10 minutes
Overview

Policies, laws and ethics shape how organisations protect data and respond to incidents.

Key concepts (expand)
  • Policies — define acceptable use, passwords, incident response, audits.
  • Legal — data protection laws (e.g., GDPR), computer misuse acts, IP law.
  • Ethical — responsible disclosure, respect for privacy, avoid misuse of vulnerabilities.
Examples
  • Policy: change passwords every 90 days; enforce 2FA.
  • Legal: fines under data-protection legislation for breaches.
  • Ethical: report discovered vulnerabilities to vendor.
Teacher notes
  • Clarify difference between legal and ethical obligations.
  • Use a recent breach to discuss consequences and required legal notifications.
